Updated June 30, 2025

Under the current administration, Temporary Protected Status (TPS) applicants and holders face increased enforcement risks, including the termination of TPS and/or removal. The Secretary of the Department of Homeland Security (DHS) has stripped thousands of individuals of TPS, terminating or advancing the end-date of several TPS designations. Litigation is ongoing with respect to each agency action. 2

Other TPS holders and applicants also are now at a heightened risk of unlawful removal. This advisory summarizes the rights of TPS holders and applicants as an aid for practitioners. It also identifies the adverse decisions DHS Secretary Noem has made to date with respect to TPS designations and the lawsuits challenging these decisions.

Following a grant of TPS, the individual 'shall not [be] remove[d]' from the United States. 8 U.S.C. § 1254a(a)(1)(A). This protection lasts for as long as the individual holds TPS. Id . Where DHS attempts to execute a final order of removal against a TPS holder, a practitioner should alert DHS to the person's status and their statutory protection against removal. Practitioners can also consider filing a habeas petition where removal is threatened or imminent.

In Sosa Ventura , the BIA also concluded that termination of removal proceedings on the basis that the respondent had been granted TPS was not proper, instead finding that a case could be administratively closed. Id. at 396. 3 However, a later-adopted regulation gives an immigration judge (IJ) discretionary authority to terminate removal proceedings on the basis that the noncitizen holds TPS. 8 C.F.R. § 1003.18(d)(1)(ii)(C). Consequently, if removal proceedings are initiated against a TPS holder, a practitioner can move to terminate the proceedings and, if necessary, argue that the regulation now controls the issue of termination.

A TPS holder also 'shall not be detained . . . on the basis of the [noncitizen's] immigration status in the United States.' 8 U.S.C. § 1254a(d)(4). Practitioners can consider filing a habeas petition on behalf of a detained TPS holder where there is a basis for claiming that the detention is due to the person's immigration status. In response, the respondent may claim that the detention is on some other basis. See, e.g., Arias Gudino v. Lowe , No. 1:25CV-00571, 2025 WL 1162488, *7 (M.D. Penn. Apr. 21, 2025) (finding petitioner had a low likelihood of success in showing detention was unlawful under § 1254a(d)(4) where respondent argued that detention was due to a violation of an order of supervision).

A TPS holder is entitled to employment authorization throughout the period that the individual holds TPS. 8 U.S.C. § 1254(a)(1)(B), (2).

A TPS holder may travel abroad with the prior consent of USCIS. 8 U.S.C. § 1245a(f)(3). Historically, USCIS authorized travel abroad for TPS holders in the form of advance parole. See NILA Practice Advisory Frequently Asked Questions: Recission of Matter of ZR-Z-C(Updated Mar. 2025) (FAQ), at 2. Beginning July 1, 2022, USCIS authorizes TPS travel using Form I-512T, Authorization for Travel by a [Noncitizen] to the United States. Id . at 4. TPS applicants must still apply for such authorization on Form I-131, Application for Travel Documents, Parole Documents, and Arrival/Departure Records. Id . at 5.

Also beginning on July 1, 2022, DHS treats a TPS holder's entry into the United States following authorized travel as an 'inspection and admission,' provided that the TPS holder meets certain requirements. Id. at 3-4. This admission satisfies the requirement that a noncitizen must have been 'inspected and admitted or paroled' for adjustment of status under 8 U.S.C. § 1255(a). Id . Additionally, a TPS holder admitted in these circumstances will be considered present in the United States pursuant to a lawful admission for purposes of 8 U.S.C. § 1255(k). Id . at 4.

Once a TPS applicant establishes 'a prima facie case of eligibility' for TPS, DHS 'shall not remove the [noncitizen] from the United States' while the application is pending. 8 U.S.C. § 1254a(a)(1)(A), (4)(B). USCIS is responsible for making the prima facie eligibility determination, just as it is responsible for making a final determination on any TPS application. However, TPS applicants generally are not informed when USCIS has made a prima facie eligibility determination.

There are reports of DHS officers removing or attempting to remove individuals with pending TPS applications. Where a client with a pending application is threatened with removal due to a final order of removal, practitioners should alert ICE to the existence of the TPS application and argue that the applicant is prima facie eligible for TPS-that is, that they satisfy the eligibility requirements for TPS, including that they are admissible or subject to an inadmissibility ground that can be waived, and that none of the statutory bars to TPS eligibility apply to them. See 8 U.S.C. § 1254a(c)(1)(A), (2).

Practitioners can also file a habeas petition to attempt to stop the removal. Note, however, that in at least one case, following the filing of a habeas petition to halt the removal, USCIS reportedly determined that the TPS applicant was not prima facie eligible for TPS and submitted a declaration from a USCIS officer stating this in the habeas proceeding.

As is true of a grant of TPS, a pending TPS application establishing prima facie eligibility does not protect the applicant from being placed in removal proceedings. See Question 1.a., supra . For applicants in removal proceedings, the BIA held, on June 6, 2025, that a pending application for TPS generally will not warrant administrative closure of a removal case, relying in part on the fact that 'TPS is a collateral benefit that does not impact the outcome of removal proceedings' and that 'the respondent would remain eligible for TPS even with a final order of removal.' Matter of B-N-K, 29 I. &amp; N. Dec. 96, 101 (BIA 2025). B-N-Kdoes not absolutely prohibit administrative closure where a TPS application is pending, and practitioners can attempt to distinguish their case on the facts. Moreover, practitioners also can move to terminate. 4 Note that B-N-Kdoes not address individuals granted TPS. See Question 1.a., supra .

4 A practitioner could argue that, while a pending TPS application is not expressly specified as a basis for discretionary termination, it is a 'comparable' circumstance to those listed in 8 C.F.R. § 1003.18(d)(1)(ii)(A)-(E) and, thus, that an IJ has discretion to terminate under § 1003.18(d)(1)(ii)(F).

Practitioners should advise their TPS applicant clients of this protection against removal. All TPS applicants-regardless of whether they are in proceedings or under a final ordermay want to carry with them a copy of the receipt notice showing that they have filed a TPS application.

Once a TPS applicant establishes 'a prima facie case of eligibility' for TPS, the applicant is entitled to an employment authorization document (EAD) while the application is pending. 8 U.S.C. § 1254a(a)(1)(B), (4)(B). USCIS is responsible for determining if an application demonstrates prima facie eligibility. Historically, even where an individual sought an EAD while their TPS application was pending, USCIS did not make a prima facie eligibility determination separate from its final determination on the merits of a TPS application. Instead, USCIS would only provide an EAD on the basis of a pending TPS application if it could not immediately approve or deny the TPS application during its adjudication of the application on the merits-that is, if it needed additional information from the applicant. As a result, the vast majority of TPS applicants never received this statutory benefit. NILA, Northwest Immigrant Rights Project and Kurzban, Kurzban, Tetzeli &amp; Pratt challenged this policy in a pending nationwide class action, Mansor v. USCIS .

After the case was filed, USCIS implemented a purportedly 'streamlined' process for determining whether a TPS application demonstrated prima facie eligibility for purposes of granting an EAD. However, in February 2025, while cross motions for summary judgment were pending before the court, USCIS paused this streamlined process. For updates about the case, see Mansor v. USCIS .

TPS applicants do not have a statutory right to travel abroad. Applicants may apply for advance parole to travel abroad. However, as is true for all who travel on advance parole, U.S. Customs and Border Protection (CBP) officers will inspect them upon return and there is no guarantee that they will be paroled into the United States. See , e.g. , 8 U.S.C. § 1225; 8 C.F.R. § 235.1(f). Consequently, clients should be advised of the risks of traveling-even with advance parole-in the current environment.

USCIS has instructed its personnel to coordinate with Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) and CBP officers regarding the timing of the issuance of any Notice to Appear-the charging document for removal proceedings. 6
